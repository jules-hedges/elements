2 Cartesian links and colinks

2.1 The category of cartesian links

Definition 2.1 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let X, X', Y, Y' be objects of {\mathcal{C}}. A cartesian link \binom{f'}{f} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} in {\mathcal{C}} is a pair of morphisms f : X \to Y and f : X \times X' \to Y'.

Example 2.1 Let f : \mathbb R^m \to \mathbb R^n be a smooth function between Euclidean spaces. There is a cartesian link D (f) : \binom{\mathbb R^m}{\mathbb R^m} \to \binom{\mathbb R^n}{\mathbb R^n} in the category \mathbf{Euc} of Euclidean spaces and smooth functions, called the forward derivative link of f, given by

  • The [what] is f : \mathbb R^m \to \mathbb R^n
  • The [what] is f' : \mathbb R^m \times \mathbb R^m \to \mathbb R^n given by f' (x, v) = v \cdot J_f (x), where J_f (x) is the Jacaobian of f evaluated at x, and \cdot is multiplication of that matrix by the vector v.

Proposition 2.1 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a cartesian closed category. Then a colink \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} can be equivalently seen as a morphism X \to Y \times [X', Y'] in {\mathcal{C}}.

Proof. Writing \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \left( \binom{X'}{X}, \binom{Y'}{Y} \right) for the set of links \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y}, there are natural isomorphisms \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \left( \binom{X'}{X}, \binom{Y'}{Y} \right) = {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y) \times {\mathcal{C}} (X \times X', Y') \cong {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y) \times {\mathcal{C}} (X, [X', Y']) \cong {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y \times [X', Y'])

Definition 2.2 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let \binom{f'}{f} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} and \binom{g'}{g} : \binom{Y'}{Y} \to \binom{Z'}{Z} be cartesian links in {\mathcal{C}}. The composition of links is given by \binom{f'}{f} \binom{g'}{g} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Z'}{Z} where

  • The [what] is f g : X \to Z
  • The [what] is (f g)' : X \times X' \to Z' given by this diagram

Proposition 2.2 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let f : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y}, g : \binom{Y'}{Y} \to \binom{Z'}{Z} and h : \binom{Z'}{Z} \to \binom{W'}{W} be cartesian links in {\mathcal{C}}. Then (\binom{f'}{f} \binom{g'}{g}) \binom{h'}{h} = \binom{f'}{f} (\binom{g'}{g} \binom{h'}{h}).

Definition 2.3 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let X, X' be objects of {\mathcal{C}}. The identity cartesian link 1_{\binom{X'}{X}} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{X'}{X} is given by

  • The [what] is the identity morphism 1_X : X \to X
  • The [what] is the projection \pi_2 : X \times X' \to X'

Proposition 2.3 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let f : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} be a cartesian link in {\mathcal{C}}. Then 1_{\binom{X'}{X}} f = f = f 1_{\binom{Y'}{Y}}.

Proof. In both cases, the first passes [fix name] are equal by uniltality in {\mathcal{C}}. For the second passes:

  • Right unitality: the composition is depicted by this diagram, which is equal to g' since f is causal and copy-delete is a comonoid.

Definition 2.4 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. Then \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) is the category whose objects are pairs of objects of {\mathcal{C}}, and whose morphisms are cartesian links in {\mathcal{C}}.

2.2 The category of cartesian colinks

Definition 2.5 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let X, X', Y, Y' be objects of {\mathcal{C}}. A cartesian colink \binom{f'}{f} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} in {\mathcal{C}} is a pair of morphisms f : X \to Y and f' : X \times Y' \to X'. f is called the forwards pass and f' is called the backwards pass.

Example 2.2 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let X, Y be objects of {\mathcal{C}}. There is a cartesian colink \pi_1 : \binom{X \times Y}{X \times Y} \to \binom{X}{X}, also known as a lens X \to Y, given by:

  • The forwards pass is the projection \pi_1 : X \times Y \to X
  • The backwards pass is \pi_1' : (X \times Y) \times X \to X \times Y given by \pi_1' ((x, y), x') = (x', y) [TODO - write with string diagram]

Similarly there is a cartesian colink \pi_2 : \binom{X \times Y}{X \times Y} \to \binom{Y}{Y}.

Example 2.3 Let f : \mathbb R^m \to \mathbb R^n be a smooth function between Euclidean spaces. There is a cartesian colink D (f) : \binom{\mathbb R^m}{\mathbb R^m} \to \binom{\mathbb R^n}{\mathbb R^n} in the category \mathbf{Euc} of Euclidean spaces and smooth functions, called the reverse derivative colink of f, given by

  • The forwards pass is f : \mathbb R^m \to \mathbb R^n
  • The backrwards pass is f' : \mathbb R^m \times \mathbb R^n \to \mathbb R^n given by f' (x, v) = J_f (x)^\top \cdot v, where J_f (x)^\top is the transpose Jacobian of f evaluated at x, and \cdot is multiplication of that matrix by the vector v.

Proposition 2.4 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a cartesian closed category. Then a colink \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} in {\mathcal{C}} can be equivalently seen as a morphism X \to Y \times [Y', X'] in {\mathcal{C}}.

Proof. Writing \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \left( \binom{X'}{X}, \binom{Y'}{Y} \right) for the set of colinks \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y}, there are natural isomorphisms \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \left( \binom{X'}{X}, \binom{Y'}{Y} \right) = {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y) \times {\mathcal{C}} (X \times Y', X') \cong {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y) \times {\mathcal{C}} (X, [Y', X']) \cong {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y \times [Y', X'])

Definition 2.6 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let \binom{f'}{f} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} and \binom{g'}{g} : \binom{Y'}{Y} \to \binom{Z'}{Z} be cartesian colinks in {\mathcal{C}}. The composition of the colinks is given by \binom{f'}{f} \binom{g'}{g} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Z'}{Z} where

  • The forwards pass is f g : X \to Z
  • The backwards pass is (f g)' : X \times Z' \to X' defined in the internal language of {\mathcal{C}} by (f g)' (x, z) = f' (x, g' (f (x), z)) [TODO - write this definition in string diagrams instead]

Proposition 2.5 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let \binom{f'}{f} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y}, \binom{g'}{g} : \binom{Y'}{Y} \to \binom{Z'}{Z} and \binom{h'}{h} : \binom{Z'}{Z} \to \binom{W'}{W} be cartesian colinks in {\mathcal{C}}. Then (\binom{f'}{f} \binom{g'}{g}) \binom{h'}{h} = \binom{f'}{f} (\binom{g'}{g} \binom{h'}{h}).

Definition 2.7 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let X, X' be objects of {\mathcal{C}}. The identity cartesian colink 1_{\binom{X'}{X}} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{X'}{X} is given by

  • The forwards pass is the identity morphism 1_X : X \to X
  • The backwwards pass is the projection \pi_2 : X \times X' \to X'

Proposition 2.6 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let f : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} be a cartesian colink in {\mathcal{C}}. Then 1_{\binom{X'}{X}} f = f = f 1_{\binom{Y'}{Y}}.

Definition 2.8 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. Then \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) is the category whose objects are pairs of objects of {\mathcal{C}}, and whose morphisms are cartesian colinks in {\mathcal{C}}.

Proposition 2.7 There is a functor D : \mathbf{Euc} \to \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}(\mathbf{Euc}) given on objects by D (X) = \binom{X}{X} and on morphisms by D (f) from example 2.3.

Proposition 2.16 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. Let \left< \mathbb E, \mathbb L \right> : \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \to {\mathcal{C}} \times {\mathcal{C}} be the functor given by \binom{X'}{X} \mapsto (X, X \times X'). Proof. There are natural isomorphisms ({\mathcal{C}} \times {\mathcal{C}})^\mathrm{op}((X, [X, X']), (Y, Y')) = {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y) \times {\mathcal{C}} (Y', [X, X']) \cong {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y) \times {\mathcal{C}} (X \times Y', X') = \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \left( \binom{X'}{X}, \binom{Y'}{Y} \right)

2.4 Products and coproducts of cartesian links and colinks

Proposition 2.18 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite (all) products. Then \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) has finite (all) products given on objects by \binom{X'}{X} \times \binom{Y'}{Y} = \binom{Y \times Y'}{X \times X'}, respectively \prod_{i : I} \binom{X'_i}{X_i} = \binom{\prod_{i : I} X'_i}{\prod_{i : I} X_i}.

Proposition 2.19 Let {\mathcal{C}} with a category with finite products whose terminal object is 1. Then \binom{1}{1} is terminal in \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}).

Proposition 2.20 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite (all) products and finite (all) distributive coproducts. Then \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) has finite (all) products given on objects by \binom{X'}{X} \times \binom{Y'}{Y} = \binom{X' + Y'}{X \times Y}, respectively \prod_{i : I} \binom{X'_i}{X_i} = \binom{\sum_{i : I} X'_i}{\prod_{i : I} X_i}.

Proposition 2.21 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, with an initial object 0 and a terminal object 1. Then \binom{0}{1} is terminal in \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}).

Proposition 2.22 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a cartesian closed category and Z an object of {\mathcal{C}}. Let R_Z : \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \to {\mathcal{C}} be the functor defined on objects by R_Z \binom{X'}{X} = X \times [Z, X']. [todo - is there a better way to define it?] Then \binom{Z}{-} \dashv R_Z.

Proof. There are natural isomorphisms \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \left( \binom{Z}{X}, \binom{Y'}{Y} \right) \cong {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y \times [Z, Y']) = {\mathcal{C}} (X, R_Z \binom{Y'}{Y})

Proposition 2.23 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a cartesian closed category and Z an object of {\mathcal{C}}. Let R_Z : \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \to {\mathcal{C}} be the functor defined on objects by R_Z \binom{X'}{X} = X \times [X', Z]. Then \binom{Z}{-} \dashv R_Z.

Proof. There are natural isomorphisms \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \left( \binom{Z}{X}, \binom{Y'}{Y} \right) \cong {\mathcal{C}} (X, Y \times [Y', Z]) = {\mathcal{C}} (X, R_Z \binom{Y'}{Y})

Proposition 2.24 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products and finite (all) coproducts. Then \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) has finite (all) coproducts that have the form \binom{Z}{X} + \binom{Z}{Y} = \binom{Z}{X + Y}, respectively \sum_{i : I} \binom{Z}{X_i} = \binom{Z}{\sum_{i : I} X_i}.

Proposition 2.25 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products and finite (all) distributive coproducts. Then \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) has finite (all) coproducts that have the form \binom{Z}{X} + \binom{Z}{Y} = \binom{Z}{X + Y}, respectively \sum_{i : I} \binom{Z}{X_i} = \binom{Z}{\sum_{i : I} X_i}.

In the case that {\mathcal{C}} is cartesian closed and has coproducts (which are automatically distributive) we get a different proof using the fact that \binom{Z}{-} : {\mathcal{C}} \to \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) preserves colimits.

Proposition 2.26 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products and an initial object 0. Then for any object X of {\mathcal{C}}, \binom{X}{0} is initial in \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}).

Proposition 2.27 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products and finite (all) distributive coproducts. Then the coproduct of \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) distributes over the product: (\binom{Z}{A} + \binom{Z}{B}) \times \binom{C'}{C} \cong (\binom{Z}{A} \times \binom{C'}{C}) + (\binom{Z}{B} \times \binom{C'}{C})

2.5 The tensor product of cartesian colinks

Definition 2.9 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and let \binom{f'}{f} : \binom{X'}{X} \to \binom{Y'}{Y} and \binom{g'}{g} : \binom{Y'}{Y} \to \binom{W'}{W} be cartesian colinks in {\mathcal{C}}. Their tensor product is the colink \binom{f'}{f} \otimes \binom{g'}{g} : \binom{X' \times Z'}{X \times Z} \to \binom{Y' \times W'}{Y \times W} given as follows:

  • The forwards pass is given by f \times g : X \times Z \to Y \times W
  • The backwards pass is given by (f \times g)' : (X \times Z) \times (Y' \times W') \to X' \times Z', (f \times g)' ((x, z), (y', w')) = (f' (x, y'), g' (z, w')) [todo write with string diagrams]

Proposition 2.28 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. The tensor product of colinks makes \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) into a symmetric monoidal category, given on objects by pairwise cartesian product. The monoidal unit is I = \binom{1}{1}.

Definition 2.10 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products.

  • We write the functor \binom{1}{-} : {\mathcal{C}} \to \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) without notation. Objects and colinks in the image of this functor are called purely covariant
  • We write -^* for the functor \binom{-}{1} : {\mathcal{C}}^\mathrm{op}\to \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}). Objects and colinks in the image of this functor are called purely contravariant

Proposition 2.29 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. Every object of \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) can be written as the tensor product of a purely covariant and a purely contravariant object, namely \binom{X'}{X} \cong X \otimes X'^*

Definition 2.11 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. For every object X of {\mathcal{C}}, there is a colink \varepsilon_X : \binom{X}{X} \to I, called a counit, given by:

  • The forwards pass is the unique morphism X \to 1
  • The backwards pass is the identity morphism X \times 1 \to X

2.6 Fibrational structure of cartesian links and colinks

Proposition 2.32 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and X an object of {\mathcal{C}}. Then the functor X \times - : {\mathcal{C}} \to {\mathcal{C}} is a comonad on {\mathcal{C}}.

Definition 2.12 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and X an object of {\mathcal{C}}. We write {\mathcal{C}} [X] for the cokleisli category of the X \times - comonad.

Proposition 2.33 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and X an object of {\mathcal{C}}. Then {\mathcal{C}} [X] has finite products.

Proposition 2.34 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products, and f : X \to Y a morphism of {\mathcal{C}}. Then f induces a functor f^* : {\mathcal{C}} [Y] \to {\mathcal{C}} [X].

Proposition 2.35 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. Then the functor \mathbb E : \overrightarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \to {\mathcal{C}} is a fibration. For each object X of {\mathcal{C}}, the fibre \mathbb E^{-1} (X) is isomorphic to {\mathcal{C}} [X], and for each morphism f : X \to Y of {\mathcal{C}}, the reindexing functor \mathbb E^{-1} (f) : {\mathcal{C}} [Y] \to {\mathcal{C}} [X] is given by f^*.

Proposition 2.36 Let {\mathcal{C}} be a category with finite products. Then the forwards pass functor \mathbb F: \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) \to {\mathcal{C}} is a fibration. For each object X of {\mathcal{C}}, the fibre \mathbb F^{-1} (X) of \overleftarrow{\mathscr L}({\mathcal{C}}) over X is isomorphic to {\mathcal{C}} [X]^\mathrm{op}, and for each morphism f : X \to Y of {\mathcal{C}}, the reindexing functor \mathbb F^{-1} (f) : {\mathcal{C}} [Y]^\mathrm{op}\to {\mathcal{C}} [X]^\mathrm{op} is given by f^*.
